History of yoga and meditation

 

☆ Pre-Vedic Era

The pre-Vedic era, dating back to around 3000 B.C., is evidenced by archaeological finds such as fossils, figurines, and seals depicting various yoga asanas, pranayama, and dhyana techniques. According to the Adiyogi legend, Lord Shiva, as the first Yoga Guru, imparted yoga knowledge to the seven great sages (Saptarishi) at Lake Kantisarovar, who then spread it worldwide. Excavations from the Indus Valley Civilization support this legend. The Saptarishi’s efforts helped integrate yoga into daily life and culture across societies, revealing similarities among global cultures of that time. Primordial Yoga, mystical in origin, thrived in South Asia during this period, alongside Tantra Yoga, which emphasized spiritual practices and progression, as shown by ancient seals and paintings.

☆ Vedic Era

During the Vedic era (circa 3000–4000 B.C.), several important Yoga-related texts emerged alongside the sacred Vedas, composed in Sanskrit. Maharishi Vyasa compiled the four Vedas—Rigveda, Yajurveda, Samveda, and Atharvaveda—with the Rigveda being the oldest and first to mention Yoga. These texts outline ethics, philosophies, rituals, and hymns aimed at leading a righteous life and achieving spiritual progress. The Vedas emphasize Yoga as a practice to harmonize mental, physical, and spiritual health, highlighting its role in spiritual enlightenment during that period.

☆ Pre-Classical Era

The Vedas are among the earliest scriptures to explain Yoga’s religious and spiritual purposes. Following them, the Upanishads focus on surrendering to the Universe’s absolute power. During the Pre-Classical Era, key Sanskrit texts—including the Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita, Puranas, Smritis, and certain Jain and Buddhist scriptures—highlight Yoga’s benefits. The Bhagavad Gita offers the most detailed insights, especially on Karma Yoga (actions and consequences) and Dnyan Yoga (knowledge). This era also includes the Ramayana, where Lord Hanuman’s devoted worship of Lord Rama exemplifies Bhakti Yoga (the Yoga of devotion).

☆ Classical Era

The Classical Era (100 B.C. to 500 A.D.) was a pivotal period for Yogic philosophy. Sage Patanjali authored the influential Yoga Sutras, outlining the Eight Limbs of Yoga and their impact on life. During this time, emphasis grew on asanas (postures), pranayama (breath control), and meditation as essential practices. There was increasing awareness of maintaining a healthy body and mind to achieve higher spiritual well-being.

☆ Post-Classical Era

The post-Classical era marked the expansion and popularization of Yoga, with scholars adapting its teachings. Influenced strongly by the Upanishads, the era emphasized the unity of soul and body and highlighted Yoga’s role in achieving detachment and enlightenment. Various schools, including Hatha Yoga and Vedanta, emerged, each with unique approaches but all rooted in Yoga’s core ancient principles. People increasingly practiced Yoga for its holistic physical and mental benefits.

☆ Modern Era

The Modern Era of Yoga, spanning roughly 150 years, has witnessed unprecedented global growth. Yoga has become a universal tool to combat modern stress and enhance well-being. Influential figures like Swami Kuvalayananda, Shri Aurobindo, Swami Mahesh Yogi, B.K.S. Iyengar, and Pattabhi Jois contributed significantly to its worldwide recognition. While modern practice often emphasizes physical fitness and mental relaxation, efforts are underway to reintroduce Yoga’s original spiritual and holistic dimensions. This ongoing evolution reflects society’s changing needs and highlights Yoga’s enduring legacy as a path toward balance, discipline, and spiritual unity.

History of Home Remedies

☆ Ancient and Global Usage

For centuries across countries, people have turned to traditional healers, home remedies, and ancient medicinal knowledge to address their health and well-being needs. According to the WHO Global Report on Traditional and Complementary Medicine (2019), various systems of traditional medicine used globally include acupuncture, herbal medicines, indigenous traditional medicine, homeopathy, traditional Chinese medicine, naturopathy, chiropractic, osteopathy, Ayurvedic and Unani medicine. One hundred and seventy WHO Member States have reported on the use of traditional medicine by their populations. Around 40% of pharmaceutical products today draw from nature and traditional knowledge.

☆ Vedic Era (1500 BCE - 500 BCE)

During this period, herbal remedies were mentioned in ancient texts like the Rigveda and Atharvaveda, which contain hymns and prayers aimed at curing diseases. Medicinal plants like Neem, Turmeric, and Ashwagandha were revered for their therapeutic properties. The Atharvaveda, in particular, is considered a treasure trove of knowledge on herbal medicine, incantations, and healing rituals.

☆ Classical Era (500 BCE - 500 CE)

The Classic Era of Home Remedies refers to a time before the advent of modern medicine, when people relied on natural and readily available substances to treat common ailments. This period, deeply rooted in ancient traditions and cultures such as Ayurveda, Traditional Chinese Medicine, and folk healing practices, emphasized the use of herbs, spices, and household ingredients like turmeric, ginger, honey, neem, and aloe vera. Remedies were passed down orally through generations, often guided by elders or traditional healers. These treatments focused on restoring balance in the body and preventing illness rather than just curing symptoms. The classic era of home remedies highlights a time when healthcare was holistic, accessible, and closely tied to nature, many of which still influence modern natural treatments today.

☆ Modern Era (1500 CE – Present)

In recent times, Ayurveda has gained global recognition, with the World Health Organization (WHO) acknowledging its value. Modern research has validated the efficacy of many Ayurvedic remedies, such as:
  • Turmeric: anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties
  • Ashwagandha: adaptogenic properties, stress relief, and improved sleep
  • Neem: antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties
  • Triphala: gentle detoxifier, improves gut health
  • Brahmi: enhances memory and cognitive function
History of Ayurveda

History of Ayurveda

☆ Ancient Origins (5000 BCE - 1500 BCE)

Ayurveda's roots can be traced back to the Indus Valley Civilization, where ancient wisdom and natural healing practices were likely passed down through oral traditions. The Vedas, ancient Hindu scriptures, contain references to medicinal plants, diseases, and treatments, showcasing the early understanding of health and wellness.

☆ Vedic Period (1500 BCE - 500 BCE)

The Atharvaveda and Rigveda mention various medicinal plants, rituals, and healing practices, laying the foundation for Ayurvedic principles. This period saw the development of a holistic approach to health, emphasizing the interconnectedness of body, mind, and spirit.

☆ Classical Period (500 BCE - 500 CE)

This era saw the development of foundational Ayurvedic texts:
  • Charaka Samhita: A comprehensive guide to Ayurvedic medicine, covering topics like diagnosis, treatment, and prevention.
  • Sushruta Samhita: A surgical text that describes various surgical procedures, showcasing the advanced medical knowledge of the time.

☆ Modern Era (1947 CE – Present)

After India's independence, Ayurveda experienced a resurgence, with efforts to standardize and promote traditional practices. Today, Ayurveda is recognized as a traditional system of medicine, with growing global popularity. Its emphasis on natural healing, prevention, and balance resonates with people seeking holistic approaches to health and wellness.

Ayurveda's history reflects its evolution as a holistic system of medicine, adapting to changing times while retaining its core principles. Its rich heritage and timeless wisdom continue to inspire new generations of practitioners and seekers of natural health.
